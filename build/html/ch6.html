Present Value vs Future ValueÂ¶

\[\begin{split}FV &= PV + rPV \\ &= PV(1+r) \\\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}FV_{t=2} &= PV(1+r)^2 \\\end{split}\]

Future Value of Investement

\[FV_{t=n} = PV(1+r)^n\]
  • n = the number of compounding periods
  • PV = the present value of investment (capital)
  • \(FV_{t=n}\) is the future value of an investment at the nth period

Note

  • r and n should talk in terms of the same time frame

    • e.g. if r is a per annum percentage, then n should be # years
    • e.g. if r is a monthly rate, then n should be # months

Effective Annual RateÂ¶

  • Effective Annual Rate (EAR), the interest rate as if it were compounded yearly instead of its actual compounding period

Example:

invest                      $100
each half year get          10%
after 1/2yr you will get    $10
at t = 1 (year)             $121

effective half year return = 10%
eff 1 year return = 21% = EAR = effective annual rate

Effective Interest Rate For 1/n YearÂ¶

Effective Rate for 1/n Year

\[\frac {j_n} {n} = eff rate for 1/n year\]
  • Annual Percentage Rate (APR), an interest rate that is expressed as an EAR for an interest rate compounded twice per year (in Canada), often used for expressing the interest rate on items like car financing, etc.

Example:

if
20% p.a. (per annum) compounded twice a year
APR - compounded twice per year in Canada
j_2 = 20%

    J implies per year
    2 implies compounded twice per year

Converting Between Rates

\[EffRate_A = (1 + EffRate_B)^p - 1\]
  • p = number of A periods in an A period

PerpetuityÂ¶

  • perpetuity, a constant, regular stream of cash flows continuing forever and starting in exactly one period

    • constant -> same amount of cash flows every period
    • regular -> periods are regular

Calculating the PV of perpetuity cash flows

Suppose cash flow every period = C

\[\begin{split}PV = \frac {C} {1 + r} + \frac {C} {(1 + r)^2} + \frac {C} {(1 + r)^3} + ... (1) \\ PV(1+r) = PV + rPV = C + \frac {C} {1 + r} + \frac {C} {(1 + r)^2} + ... (2) \\ \\ rPV = C (1 - 2) \\ \\ PV = \frac {C} {r}\end{split}\]

Example 1Â¶

::
r = 10%, C = $1000 p.a, starting at t=1
\[\begin{split}PV_{t>=1} &= \frac {C} {r} \\ &= 1000/0.01 \\ &= 10000\end{split}\]

Example 2Â¶

r = 10%,
C = $1000 p.a, starting at t=0

Method 1

\[PV_{t>=0} = PV_{1000} + PV_{t>=1}\]

Method 2

\[\begin{split}PV_{t>=-1} &= \frac {C} {r} \\ \\ PV_{t>=0} &= PV_{t>=-1} * (1 + r) \\ &= 1000/0.1 * (1 * 0.1) \\ &= 10000\end{split}\]

Example 3Â¶

r = 10%, C = $1000 p.a, starting at t=0.5

\[\begin{split}PV_{t>=-0.5} &= \frac {C} {r} \\ \\ PV_{t>=0} &= PV_{t>=-0.5} * (1 + r)^{0.5} \\ &= 1000/0.1 * (1 * 0.1) \\ &= 10000\end{split}\]

In generalÂ¶

\[\begin{split}if \\ PV_{t>=-n} = \frac {C} {r}, n > 0 \\ \\ then \\ PV_{t>=0} = PV_{t>=n) * (1 + r)^(n)\end{split}\]

AnnuityÂ¶

  • annuity, a constant, regular, finite cash flow stream starting in exactly one period

    • difference between Annuity and Perpetuity is that the cash flow ends at some point
\[\begin{split}PV_{1<=t<=n} &= PV_{t>=1} - PV_{t>=n} \\ &= \frac {C} {r} - \frac {\frac {C} {r}} {(1+r)^n} \\ &= \frac {C} {r} * (1 - \frac {1} {(1+r)^n}) \\ \\ where, \\ \\ n = \text{number of cash flow periods}\end{split}\]

ExampleÂ¶

r = 10%
Annual cash flows starting in 1 yr
n = 20
\[\begin{split}PV &= \frac {C} {r} * (1 - \frac {1} {(1+r)^n}) \\ &= \frac 1000 0.1 * (1 - \frac 1 {1.1^20} \\ &= 8513\end{split}\]

\[\begin{split}PV &= \frac {C} {r} * (1 - \frac {1} {(1+r)^n})*(1+r) \\ &= \frac {1000} {0.1} * (1 - \frac 1 {1.1^{21}}) * 1.1 \\ &= 9513.56\end{split}\]

growth perpetuityÂ¶

\[\begin{split}\text{if } g < r \\ \text{then } P = \frac C {r-g} \\ \\ \text{if } g > r \\ \text{then P doesn't converge} \\\end{split}\]

growth annuityÂ¶

\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}PV = \frac C {r-g} * (1-(\frac {1+g} {1+r})^n)\\\text {as } n \rightarrow \infty , (\frac {1+g} {1+r})^n \rightarrow 0 , \text{ and } PV = \frac C {r-g}\end{aligned}\end{align} \]
